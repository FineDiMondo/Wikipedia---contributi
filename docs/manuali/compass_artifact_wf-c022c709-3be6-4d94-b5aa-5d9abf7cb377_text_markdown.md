# Gallica Archive Search: Sicilian Heritage Collection

Nine Gallica items have been identified and verified for this digital archive dataset, spanning maps of the Kingdom of Sicily, European and Spanish heraldic armorials, historical views of Palermo, and documents on the Spanish viceregal system. **All items are confirmed public domain** (pre-1900 publication dates or centuries-old manuscripts), and every ark URL has been cross-verified through web search against live Gallica listings. Three items were flagged for REVIEW due to indirect matching on family-specific heraldry.

## Findings summary by cluster

### MAPPE_SICILIA (Rows 1–2)

Two strong cartographic items were secured. **Row 1** features Nicolas de Fer's *Isle et royaume de Sicile* (1705), a complete engraved map of the entire Kingdom of Sicily from the BnF's Cartes et plans department. An excellent alternative exists in Guillaume Delisle's 1717 map (ark: btv1b59719534), another verified item by one of France's most important Enlightenment-era cartographers. **Row 2** yielded a 16th-century map/view of Trapani published in Rome by Joannes Orlandii — directly relevant to the western Sicily / Belice valley region near Santa Ninfa. A broader alternative is the 1784 Schmettau-based map of all Sicily (btv1b8492822h), which shows detailed topography of the Trapani-Palermo corridor.

### ARALDICA (Rows 3–4)

The heraldic cluster produced two excellent matches. **Row 3** secured the cornerstone French armorial: d'Hozier's *Armorial général de France* (1875 publication of the 1696–1709 official registers), which is **the** definitive reference for French heraldic bearings. The original 69-volume manuscript set with **color blazon plates** is also on Gallica, organized by French provinces. **Row 4** found a remarkable manuscript: a painted armorial of the principal families of Castile, Aragon, and Catalonia (btv1b100338460), covering the period from Isabella the Catholic to Philip III. This illuminated manuscript contains actual painted heraldic shields — making it potentially the single most relevant Gallica source for all three target families (Arias, Guevara, Del Castillo) given their Castilian-Aragonese origins.

### CITTA (Row 5)

An 18th-century *Plan de la ville de Palerme* (btv1b53042288s) was identified — an anonymous city plan showing Palermo's full urban layout, matching the target period precisely. The Cassaro and port area would be visible in this plan. Two strong alternatives were found: a detailed 1780 Italian-language plan at 1:6,000 scale (btv1b53233088m) and Gustave Le Gray's 1860 panoramic photograph of Palermo (btv1b84578738).

### VICEREGNO (Row 6)

The Abbé de Saint-Non's *Voyage pittoresque* (1781–1786) was selected — a monumental 4-volume illustrated work on the Kingdoms of Naples and Sicily, one of the most important 18th-century publications about the region. It contains numerous engravings and covers the history of Spanish administration. A more text-focused alternative is Auguste de Sayve's *Voyage en Sicile* (1822, ark: bpt6k107014z), which explicitly describes the viceregal palace, the vice-roi's ceremonial role, and streets named after Spanish viceroys (Strada Maqueda, rue de Tolède).

### RETI_FAM (Rows 7–9)

All three family heraldry rows point to **Rietstap's *Armorial général*** (1884–1887, 2nd edition), the standard international reference containing blazon descriptions for over **130,000 European noble families**. Volume I (A–K, ark: bpt6k62023b) covers all three target families alphabetically: Arias (A section), Castillo (C section), Guevara (G section). The 1861 first edition (bpt6k5505571j) is also available. Both are confirmed public domain. **Critical limitation**: Rietstap provides text blazon descriptions, not illustrated plates. For actual painted coats of arms, the Spanish manuscript armorial (btv1b100338460) from Row 4 is the recommended cross-reference for all three families.

## Giardina connection status

**No Gallica source was found mentioning "Giardina," "Arias Giardina," or "Luigi Arias Giardina."** External genealogical sources confirm that Luigi Arias Giardina acquired the barony of Santa Ninfa in the Val di Mazzara in 1615 and became its first Marquis in 1621. He also held the fiefdom of Gibellini and the principality of Ficarazzi. The Giardina branch originated from Catania, Sicily. However, Rietstap's armorial lists blazons by primary family surname only and does not record feudal sub-branches or Sicilian-specific titles. **To find the Arias-Giardina connection on Gallica, a more specialized search for Sicilian nobility registers, feudal records, or works specifically about the baronage of the Val di Mazzara would be required.** None of the 9 rows currently qualifies for the "DIRECT MATCH GIARDINA" flag based on verified Gallica sources.
